Rhetorical Analysis Terms

Alliteration

  • repetition of sounds, especially initial consonant sounds in two or more neighboring words (as in “she sells sea shells”).
    • *Let us go forth to lead the land we love. J. F. Kennedy, Inaugural Address

Allusion

  • A direct or indirect reference to something which is presumably commonly known, such as an event, book, myth, place, or work of art. Allusions can be historical (like referring to Hitler), literary (like referring to Kurtz in Heart of Darkness), religious (like referring to Noah and the flood), or mythical (like referring to Atlas). There are, of course, many more possibilities, and a work may simultaneously use multiple layers of allusion. Consider why a writer chooses any particular allusion as related to purpose.

Anaphora

  • the repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive phrases, clauses or lines.
    • *. . . we shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets, we shall fight in the hills. We shall never surrender. Winston Churchill

Antistrophe

  • repetition of the same word or phrase at the end of successive clauses.
    • *In 1931, ten years ago, Japan invaded Manchukuo—without warning. In 1935, Italy invaded Ethiopia—without warning. In 1938, Hitler occupied Austria—without warning. Franklin D. Roosevelt

Antithesis

  • opposition, or contrast of ideas or words in a balanced or parallel construction.
    • *Extremism in defense of liberty is no vice, moderation in the pursuit of justice is no virtue. Barry Goldwater
    • *Brutus: Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more. Shakespeare, Julius Caesar

Aporia

  • expression of doubt (often feigned) by which a speaker appears uncertain as to what he should think, say, or do.
    • *Then the steward said within himself, 'What shall I do?' Luke 16
    • *I do not remember ever to have appeared as a speaker before any assembly ... with a greater distrust of my ability than I do this day. Frederick Douglass, Independence Day Speech

Apostrophe

  • A figure of speech that directly addresses an absent or imaginary person or a personified abstraction, such as liberty or love. The effect may add familiarity or emotional intensity.
    • *Judge, O you gods, how dearly Caesar loved him. Shakespeare, Julius Caesar
      • *William Wordsworth addresses John Milton as he writes, “Milton, thou shouldst be living at this hour: England hath need of thee.”

Audience

  • the intended readership for a piece of writing (or listeners at a speech)

Ethos

  • (appeal) to a sense of ethics/morals establishment of author’s credibility

Exigence

  • something that inspires, stimulates, provokes, or prompts the writer to create a text. (don’t use this term in your responses, just know what it means)

Hyperbole

  • exaggeration for emphasis or for rhetorical effect.
    • *. . . I hear the mournful wail of millions! Frederick Douglass, Independence Day Speech

Imagery

  • The sensory details or figurative language used to describe, arouse emotion, or represent abstractions. On a physical level, imagery uses terms related to the five senses; we refer to visual, auditory, tactile, gustatory, or olfactory imagery. Pay attention to how an author creates imagery and to the effect of that imagery.

Irony

  • The contrast between what is stated explicitly and what is really meant. The difference between what appears to be and what actually is true

Litotes

  • an understatement, for intensification
    • *War is not healthy for children and other living things.
    • *One nuclear bomb can ruin your whole day.

Logos

  • (appeal) to a sense of logic or reason

Metaphor

  • implied comparison achieved through a figurative use of words; the word is used not in its literal sense, but in one analogous to it.
    • *Life's but a walking shadow; a poor player, That struts and frets his hour upon the stage. Shakespeare, Macbeth

Metonymy

  • substitution of one word for another which it suggests- kind of metaphor- suggestion beyond or much greater than itself; far reaching- symbol of something in general, suggestive of something bigger
    • *He is a man of the cloth.
    • *The pen is mightier than the sword.
    • *By the sweat of thy brow thou shalt eat thy bread.

Oxymoron

  • a combination of two contradictory terms to suggest a paradox.
    • *jumbo shrimp
    • *cruel kindness

Paradox

  • a statement that appears to be self-contradictory or opposed to common sense but upon closer inspection contains some idea of truth or validity.
    • *What a pity that youth must be wasted on the young. George Bernard Shaw
    • *I must be cruel only to be kind. Shakespeare, Hamlet

Pathos

  • (appeal) to emotion. Always identify the specific emotion the writer/speaker is eliciting.

Personification

  • the attribution of personality to an impersonal thing.
    • *England expects every man to do his duty. Lord Nelson

Polysyndeton

  • the repetition of conjunctions in a series of coordinate words, phrases, or clauses.
    • *. . . it was dark and there was water standing in the street and no lights and windows broke and boats all up in the town and trees were blown down and everything all blown and I got a skiff and went out and found my boat . . Hemingway, “After the Storm”

Purpose (also called Rhetorical Purpose)

  • what the writer hopes to accomplish. Writers may have more than one purpose in a text.

Rhetorical Question

  • a question asked in order to create a dramatic effect or to make a point rather than to get an answer.

Rhetorical Situation

  • the context, or the culmination of events or people that create exigence (a necessity to speak out). Because rhetoric is framed around the way it will be received, the rhetorical situation influences the wording with which orators present arguments, and the arguments that are made in the first place.
  • For example, MLK’s rhetorical situation in his Letter from a Birmingham Jail was the continual discrimination in Alabama and the bystanding clergymen who are complicit in it. Their questioning of his timing and direct action being taken provided the exigence for MLK to write his letter, which he writes in response to the inaction and criticism of his peers.

Rhetorical Structure

  • This phrase refers to how a passage is constructed. If asked to consider rhetorical structure, look at the passage’s organization and how the writer combines images, details, or arguments to serve his or her purpose.

Sentence Structure

  • When an essay question asks you to analyze sentence structure, look at the type of sentences the author uses. Remember that the basic sentence structures are simple, compound, and complex, and variations are created with sentence combining. Also consider variation or lack of it in sentence length, any unusual devices in sentence construction, such as repetition or inverted word order, and any unusual word or phrase placement. As with all devices, be prepared to discuss the effect of the sentence structure. For example, a series of short, simple sentences or phrases can produce a feeling of speed and choppiness, which may suit the author’s purpose.

Simile

  • an explicit comparison between two things using 'like' or 'as'.
    • *My love is as a fever . . . Shakespeare, Sonnet CXLVII

Syllepsis

  • use of a word in two separate applications of definitions within the same phrase or statement.
    • *We must all hang together or assuredly we will all hang separately. Benjamin Franklin

Tautology

  • repetition of an idea in a different word, phrase, or sentence for emphasis.
    • *With malice toward none, with charity for all. Lincoln, Second Inaugural Address

Tone

  • the attitude of the writer toward a subject or audience. Some words that can describe tone are playful, somber, didactic, sarcastic, etc.
